2. df.describe vs individual statistical methods
- There was a class question about df.describe returning different results or multiple methods producing the same outcome.
- Instructor clarified:
  - Use the method form (with parentheses) where arguments/parameters may be provided; some attributes (e.g., shape) have no parentheses because they take no arguments.
  - df.describe is a grouped packaged calculation that works on numeric columns by default; it returns aggregated descriptive statistics.
  - Individual methods (DataFrame.mean(), .median(), .std()) can be used either on the whole DataFrame (if all columns are numeric) or on specific columns.
  - Multiple methods may yield the same outcome for the same dataset (example: describe vs individual calls on a fully numeric DataFrame).
  - If a column contains non-numeric (text/string) data, some statistical measures (mean, median, std) cannot be computed; in such cases, perform individual column-specific calculations where applicable.
  - Instructor emphasized the importance of checking the data types before applying describe() or statistical aggregations.

4. Correlation analysis (corr)
- Correlation is used to measure relationship between two datasets/columns; instructor previewed deeper statistical treatment to come in later lessons (statistics chapter, lesson 6 or 7).
- The pandas method discussed: DataFrame.corr()
  - When called on a DataFrame it computes pairwise correlation coefficients between all columns (pairwise correlation matrix)
  - Output is a matrix where diagonal entries are 1 (each column perfectly correlates with itself)
- Interpretation of correlation coefficient values:
  - Range: -1 to +1
  - Positive correlation (0 to +1): as one variable increases, the other tends to increase; higher values closer to +1 indicate stronger positive correlation (example: speed and distance with time constant)
  - Negative correlation (-1 to 0): as one variable increases, the other tends to decrease; values closer to -1 indicate stronger negative correlation (example: for constant distance, increasing time implies decreasing speed)
  - Values near 0 indicate little or no linear correlation
- Instructor walked through example correlations in the notebook and interpreted pairwise values: some pairs showed high correlation (close to 1), others low positive correlation (~0.3), diagonals were 1.0 as expected.
- Emphasis: statistical outcomes and measures (like correlation coefficients) give credibility, accountability and justification for data-science analyses and decisions.

- Instructor demonstrated that value_counts can be run on a DataFrame if it has a single column or on a specific column when DataFrame has multiple columns
- Example use case: survey of kids’ favorite fruits; counts provide frequency of each fruit label (e.g., Apple: 4, Banana: 3, Cherry: 3)
