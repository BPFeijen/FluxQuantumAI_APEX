## Discussion
### Chi-square test (test of independence)
* Demonstration: Geetika imported the chi-square package, loaded the file (pytest.csv), and created a contingency table from two categorical columns (gender and shopping). Participants were asked to upload the file and confirm readiness in chat; Barbara confirmed readiness.
* Contingency table: Explained as reorganization of categorical counts (e.g., male/female vs. yes/no) that turns categorical observations into numerical counts for statistical calculation.
* Chi-square mechanics: Covered how chi-square cutoff (critical value) is determined from the contingency table, how the chi-square statistic is compared to that cutoff, and how subsequent p-value is calculated from internal statistics including degrees of freedom.
* Interpretation: Emphasized p-value < 0.05 indicates a statistically significant association (not due to chance); p-value ≥ 0.05 indicates no significant association (retain null). In the live run the class observed p = 0.76 (retain null — independence between variables).
* Practical relevance: Linked test of independence results to downstream modeling choices (example: if categorical predictors are independent, one might consider using a Naive Bayes classifier in later ML pipeline stages). Geetika highlighted the importance of understanding these tests even if automated in later ML libraries.
* Troubleshooting: Multiple students (including Barbara) encountered import/runtime errors; Geetika provided the complete code snippet and troubleshooting guidance (import scipy.stats) and waited for confirmations (“done”) from learners as code executed.

### Inferential vs Descriptive statistics (flow revision)
* Framework: Geetika reviewed the two broad categories — descriptive (measures of central tendency, dispersion, shape, correlation and covariance) and inferential (t-test, z-test, chi-square, ANOVA).
* Emphasis: Reiterated p-value interpretation and that t-test/z-test analyze mean differences while chi-square analyzes association among categorical variables.

### One-way ANOVA (introduction and assumptions)
* Purpose: ANOVA checks whether there are significant differences among means of 3 or more independent groups; p-value < 0.05 implies at least one group mean differs significantly.
* F-statistic: Introduced as the ANOVA analog to chi-square statistic; both are part of the internal calculation leading to a p-value.
* When to use: Use t-test/z-test for 2-group mean comparisons, ANOVA for 3+ groups (one-way for a single factor; two-way when two factors are considered).
* Assumptions: Groups are independent (no association), data in each group are approximately normally distributed (no heavy skew/outliers), and group variances are similar (homogeneity of variance).
* Examples and use cases: Several real-world examples were discussed where one-way ANOVA applies: comparing student scores across three teaching methods (factor = teaching method), customer satisfaction across three store locations (factor = location/service quality), and crop yields across three fertilizer types (factor = fertilizer). For each, p < 0.05 would indicate the best mean (e.g., best teaching method) is statistically different, not due to chance.
* Non-applicable cases: Geetika noted ANOVA is inappropriate when variables are mathematically dependent (e.g., distance = speed × time) because the independence assumption is violated.
